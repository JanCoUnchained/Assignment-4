Quick overview

In this analysis, we will be implementing priors arising form meta-analyses into the Bayesian modeling framework. In order to illustrate what consequences the usage of different priors might have, we fit two models of pitch data: first one using custom weakly regularizing priors and the second one using meta-analytic priors. Said meta-analytic priors are acquired as the first step in this process: we conduct a Bayesian meta-analysis of provided studies.

Preparing the analysis

The outcome variable of interest in this assignment is the standard deviation of pitch. This variable is not normally distributed, indicating that it arises from a non-additive process. It is instead log-normally distributed. Thus we decided to transform variables of interest both in the meta-analytic data and the data from Riccardoâs study of pitch.

In order to prepare the meta-analytic data, we have drawn from a transformation formula presented by Quan & Zhang (2003). That is, we went back to the meta-analysis and calculated variance for the standard deviation of pitch (separately for schizophrenics and controls). Next, this variance is log transformed in order to make the variable approximately normally distributed, as we are fitting a model of the Gaussian family. Finally, the square root of this transformed variable is taken, giving us the standard deviation of log transformed standard deviation of the pitch.

sqrt(log(SD of SD of pitch^2 / Mean of SD of pitch^2))

Since variance is the square root of standard deviation we took the square root of the variance to obtain the logarithm of standard deviation. We then proceeded to calculate the logarithm of the mean & then used the escalc-function from the metafor-package to obtain effect size (yi) and* *variance of said effect sizes (vi).

For the data in Riccardoâs study we took the logarithm and then z-scored the outcome variable standard deviation of pitch.

This preprocessing ensures that the data from the meta-analysis and the data from Riccardoâs studies are comparable (which we need when we use the meta-analytic prior in task 4).

Making the meta-analysis

In constructing the meta-analysis we used Matti Vuorreâs framework. The model estimates effect size (yi) and weighs it by variance (vi). The studies which factor in to the meta-analysis are modeled as random effects. We chose to use the studyref column which treats each study as independent in our random effects structure. Another approach would have been to base it on author (I.e., assuming that the variation in results are due to experimenter/lab variation).

Making the model (weakly regularizing priors)

Random effects:

We have identified hierarchy in our data to be used as random intercepts; These include unique participant ID, session number (data was collected across three sessions) and trial number. Using these levels we aim to capture non-systematic variance arising from differences between participants not caused by diagnosis and from circumstances at data collection. Each participant took part in only one session and completed up to 10 trials. Although instructions in each session differed slightly, they aimed to capture the same effect, which makes differences between said sessions unsystematic in the context of our analysis.

Distribution of outcome

As the outcome variable (standard deviation of pitch) was log-transformed, our model will estimate a normal distribution on a log-scale. This means that the outcome variable is exponentially distributed on a natural scale.

The intercept (which here is the estimate of controls) follows a normal distribution on the log-scale, which is also the case for the beta (effect of diagnosis). This indicates that both processes are exponential on the natural scale.

For random effects, we had random intercepts for the different studies, participants, and trial numbers.

Priors

When setting priors, we went for weakly regularizing priors, considering the scale of the data and pushing the model towards a null result. Because the data is transformed twice, we first considered the priors on the natural sale before transforming them to the log-z-scale. Our priors for the intercept and error of the intercept were normal distributions with means of zero and standard deviations of 0.2. Our priors for the effect size (beta-estimate) and sigma were normal distributions with means of zero and standard deviations of 0.3.

Making the model (meta-analytic priors)

Overview

Our second model has the same random structure as the first one. The difference lies in the priors. While we kept the priors for the intercept, standard deviation, and sigma the same, the prior mean effect size was set to a normal distribution with a mean of -0.57 and a standard deviation of 0.25 which were the approximate effect size estimates obtained from the meta-analysis.

Bayesian reflection

When doing Bayesian analyses, we set priors that reflect our state of knowledge before evaluating the data. Our prior knowledge may be very sparse as in the case when we use weakly regularizing priors. We might incorporate expert knowledge, or we might use results from a meta-analysis. In the hierarchy of evidence, meta-analyses are on top because they reflect the hitherto accumulation of scientific evidence at a given point in time. And in principle, estimates from a meta-analysis provide the most qualified guess at the true parameter value. Especially if you are conducting a study where having many participants is infeasible, you could benefit from meta-analytic priors.

However, there are some drawbacks to using meta-analytic priors. Conducting a meta-analysis takes time, and sometimes, as exemplified in our analysis, weakly regularizing priors will do almost just as well as meta-analytic priors. Furthermore, if the quality of the studies selected for meta-analysis is low, using a meta-analytic prior can be outright misleading. This is also true if the field as a whole is subject to publication biases (e.g.Â the file-drawer problem). On the other hand, having a strong meta-analytic priors can safeguard against publication bias by forcing the models to be skeptical of flashy results. Lastly, an obvious, but nonetheless important, condition for using meta-analytic priors is that the studies in the meta-analysis are actually measuring the same thing. If the conditions of the studies are very different, e.g.Â different experimental conditions, different measured parameters etc., a meta-analysis wonât be very illuminating.

In sum, using meta-analytic priors perfectly reflect the Bayesian philosophy: âYesterdayâs posterior is todayâs prior.â They provide the strongest indication of the state of the field. Nevertheless, one should always be wary and carefully assess the quality of the studies used for the meta-analysis.
